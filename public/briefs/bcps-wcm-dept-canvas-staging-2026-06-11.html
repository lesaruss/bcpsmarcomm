Welcome to the WCM Certification - Department

Welcome, and thank you for taking the time to complete this training.

This course is designed for Broward County Public Schools (BCPS) department-level Web Content Managers (WCMs). Whether you are new to managing a department website or refreshing your skills, this certification will give you the knowledge and tools to maintain a professional, accessible, and compliant department presence on browardschools.com.

As a department WCM, you are part of a network of content stewards who help keep the BCPS website accurate, organized, and useful for everyone who depends on it, including school administrators, students, families, staff, and the general public.

Before you begin: Make sure you have your Finalsite Composer login credentials ready. You will need access to your department site to complete the hands-on activities in this course.

When you complete this course and earn your certification badge, you will have demonstrated that you can manage your department site to the standards BCPS expects. Let us get started.

Department WCMs who complete this certification are better prepared for site audits and contribute to a stronger, more consistent BCPS web presence.

Page 4 - What Is a Department WCM?

What Is a Department WCM?

A Web Content Manager (WCM) is the person responsible for keeping a department website accurate, current, and compliant with BCPS standards. You are the primary point of contact for your department's web presence.

Your Core Responsibilities

  • Maintaining accurate and up-to-date content on your department site
  • Following BCPS web standards for layout, navigation, content quality, and style
  • Ensuring all content meets ADA accessibility requirements
  • Responding to audit findings and making corrections in a timely manner
  • Coordinating with the Office of Communications on major site changes

Department WCM vs. School WCM

Department sites have higher visibility and broader impact than individual school sites. Your pages may be referenced by all 200+ schools, used in district communications, and accessed by state and federal stakeholders.

Think of your department site as the official district record for your area. Accuracy, clarity, and accessibility are not optional - they are part of the role.

Who to Contact

Your primary resource for questions about web standards, Finalsite access, and site audits is the BCPS Office of Communications. Contact information and escalation guidance are available in the Resources page of this module.

Page 1 - BCPS Department Website Standards

BCPS Department Website Standards

Every department website on browardschools.com is expected to meet the same core standards. These standards exist to ensure that all visitors to BCPS department pages receive a consistent, professional, and accessible experience.

As a department WCM, you are responsible for making sure your site meets these standards at all times, not just when it is first set up.

Why Standards Matter

When standards are applied consistently across all department sites, the results are clear:

  • Visitors can find what they need quickly and reliably
  • Your department presents a professional image that reflects well on BCPS
  • Content is accessible to all users, including those with disabilities
  • Your site is easier to audit and maintain over time

What the Standards Cover

The BCPS Department Web Standards address six areas: Layout and Structure, Content Quality, Navigation, Post Boards, Resource Elements, and Style Guidelines. Each of these areas is covered in depth in Modules 3 through 8.

These standards are maintained and updated by the Office of Communications. The most current version is always available at browardschools.com/wcm-community/departments/standards-guidelines.

Page 2 - Required Content: Homepage

Required Content: Homepage

Your department homepage is the first page most visitors will see. It sets the tone for your entire site and must include specific required elements to meet BCPS standards.

Required Homepage Elements

  • Hero section: A clear, relevant banner image or visual that represents your department. The hero should include a brief tagline or headline identifying the department.
  • Quick Access buttons: Three to six buttons linking to your most-visited or most important pages. These should reflect the services and resources your audience needs most.
  • News or Announcements section: A post board or content section featuring current news, updates, or announcements relevant to your department.
  • Footer: The BCPS standard footer with required district links and legal notices. This is maintained at the district level and should not be altered.

Organizing Your Homepage

A homepage that is too busy is just as problematic as one that is too sparse. Prioritize the information your audience needs most, and keep secondary content on interior pages.

Your homepage should answer the question: "What does this department do, and where do I go to get what I need?" Answer that question clearly and your homepage is doing its job.

Common mistake: Listing every possible link on the homepage. Limit Quick Access buttons to your top priorities. Too many options slow visitors down.

Page 3 - Homepage Sections

Homepage Sections

Beyond the required elements, your homepage may include additional content sections that highlight key programs, services, or information. The standards below apply to all homepage sections.

Section Organization

  • Each section should have a clear heading that tells visitors what they will find
  • Content within each section should be directly related to its heading
  • Sections should be ordered by priority - the most important content appears highest on the page
  • Avoid sections with only one item or very little content - consolidate where possible

Visual Balance

Your homepage should be visually balanced. A mix of images, text, and buttons helps break up the page and makes it easier to scan. Avoid pages that are all text or all images.

Keeping Sections Current

Every section on your homepage must be actively maintained. Remove or archive sections that are no longer relevant. A homepage with outdated sections undermines trust in your department site.

Review your homepage at the start of each semester to make sure all sections are current, accurate, and still serving your visitors.

Page 4 - Interior Pages

Interior Pages

Interior pages are all the pages within your site that sit below the homepage. These are where most of your department content lives, from program descriptions and forms to policies and staff directories.

Interior Page Standards

  • Clear page title: Every interior page must have a title that accurately describes its content. The title should match or closely reflect the navigation label that leads to it.
  • Logical content order: Information should be organized so the most important content appears first. Use headings to break up long pages.
  • No orphaned pages: Every page should be reachable from your site navigation or from a link on another page. Pages that cannot be found are not useful to anyone.
  • Consistent layout: Interior pages should use the same layout structure across your site. Visitors should not have to relearn the layout every time they navigate to a new section.

When to Create a New Page

Create a new interior page when you have a distinct topic or service that does not fit cleanly on an existing page. Avoid creating pages just to have more pages - consolidate related content where it makes sense.

Before creating a new page, ask: does this content belong on an existing page, or does it truly need its own space? Fewer, better-organized pages are almost always more effective than many thin pages.

Page 1 - Why Layout and Structure Matter

Why Layout and Structure Matter

Layout and structure are the foundation of a usable website. When a page is well organized, visitors can find what they need quickly. When it is not, even the most accurate and up-to-date content becomes difficult to use.

For department sites, this matters even more. Your audience includes school administrators, staff, and families who may be looking for specific policies, forms, or program information under time pressure. A clear layout removes friction and helps them get what they need.

What Structure Communicates

Good structure is not just about visual appearance. It communicates:

  • What is most important on the page
  • How different pieces of content relate to each other
  • Where a visitor should look next

Your Role

As a department WCM, you are responsible for maintaining consistent layout and structure across all pages in your site. This means applying the same organizational logic every time you create or update a page, not just when the site is first built.

A well-structured page is one that a first-time visitor can understand without needing any explanation. Use that as your test when reviewing your pages.

Page 2 - Layout Standards

Layout Standards

The following standards apply to all pages on your department site. These are not stylistic preferences - they are baseline requirements for every page you manage.

Logical Page Flow

Content on each page should follow a logical order. Lead with the most important information, then provide supporting detail. Avoid burying key facts at the bottom of a long page.

Clear Section Organization

Use headings to organize long pages into clear sections. Each heading should accurately describe the content below it. Visitors should be able to scan the headings alone and understand what the page covers.

Consistent Layout Across Pages

Your site should feel cohesive. Pages with drastically different layouts create confusion and make your site feel unmanaged. Use the same structural patterns across pages of the same type.

Balanced Elements

Avoid clustering too many elements in one area of a page. Mix text, images, and interactive elements in a way that creates visual balance and does not overwhelm the visitor.

Mobile and Responsive Design

Finalsite pages are responsive by default, but the content choices you make affect how pages display on mobile. Avoid very wide tables, oversized images, or layouts that rely heavily on side-by-side columns - these often break on small screens.

Before publishing a page, view it on a mobile device or use your browser's responsive preview mode to confirm it looks correct at smaller screen sizes.

Page 3 - Content Maintenance and Visual Consistency

Content Maintenance and Visual Consistency

A well-structured site requires ongoing maintenance. Structure degrades over time as content is added, updated, and removed without a consistent approach. Your job is to maintain the structure you inherit or build, not just populate it with content.

Maintaining Structure Over Time

  • When adding a new section, follow the same organizational pattern used elsewhere on the page
  • When removing content, check whether the surrounding structure still makes sense without it
  • When updating content, make sure headings still accurately reflect what follows them

Avoiding Content Overload

One of the most common layout problems is adding too much content to a single page. If a page is getting very long, ask whether some content should be moved to a sub-page or removed entirely.

Strategic white space - the empty space between content elements - is not wasted space. It gives the eye a place to rest and makes pages easier to read.

Visual Consistency Checklist

  • Heading levels are used consistently (H2 for major sections, H3 for sub-sections)
  • Images are similar in style and do not clash visually
  • Button styles are the same across the page
  • Text formatting (bold, italics) is used sparingly and consistently
Page 4 - Common Layout Mistakes

Common Layout Mistakes

The following are the most frequent layout and structure issues found during BCPS department site audits. Reviewing these will help you avoid them on your own site.

Walls of Text

Large blocks of unbroken text are difficult to read and often cause visitors to leave the page. Break up long content with headings, bullet points, or shorter paragraphs.

Inconsistent Heading Use

Skipping heading levels (for example, jumping from H2 directly to H4) or using headings only for style rather than structure makes pages harder to read and hurts accessibility. Use headings in order and for their intended purpose.

Too Many Elements on One Page

A page with images, post boards, resource elements, feature boxes, and callout blocks all stacked together creates visual noise. Each element competes for attention and none stands out. Choose the elements that serve your content best and use others sparingly.

Overcrowded Pages

Resist the impulse to put everything on one page. If you find yourself creating a very long page because "it's all related," consider whether the content naturally divides into separate topics that deserve their own pages.

Audit flag: Pages with more than 1,500 words of body text are frequently flagged during content audits as candidates for restructuring. Review long pages regularly.

Page 5 - Layout Quick Checklist

Layout Quick Checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing any page on your department site. If you cannot check every item, the page needs attention before it is published or left live.

  • The page has a clear, accurate title
  • The most important content appears at the top
  • Headings are used to organize the page into clear sections
  • Heading levels are used in logical order (H2, H3, not H2 then H4)
  • The layout is consistent with other pages of the same type on this site
  • There are no walls of unbroken text
  • White space is used effectively - the page does not feel cluttered
  • Content elements are balanced across the page
  • The page displays correctly on a mobile screen
  • There is no outdated or placeholder content on the page

This checklist also applies before submitting a page for review or during your regular site audit. Bookmark it for future reference.

Page 1 - Why Content Quality Matters

Why Content Quality Matters

Department content on browardschools.com is authoritative. That means your pages are the official district source of truth for your area - the information that schools, staff, and families rely on to make decisions, complete tasks, and access services.

When that content is inaccurate, outdated, or poorly written, it creates real problems. Staff may follow outdated procedures. Families may submit the wrong forms. Schools may reference information that no longer applies.

The Stakes Are Higher for Departments

A single error on a school homepage affects one school community. An error on a department page can affect all 200+ schools and thousands of families across Broward County.

What High-Quality Content Looks Like

  • Accurate and aligned with current district policy
  • Current - reflecting the most recent information available
  • Written clearly for multiple audiences
  • Structured so visitors can find what they need quickly
  • Free of formatting errors, typos, and broken links
  • Accessible to all users, including those using assistive technology

If you are unsure whether content is still accurate or current, do not leave it as-is. Remove it, replace it with a note that the information is being updated, or contact the appropriate person in your department to verify it.

Page 2 - Writing Standards

Writing Standards for Department Sites

The way content is written affects how useful it is. These writing standards apply to all text content on your department site.

Audience Awareness

Department sites serve multiple audiences: school administrators, teachers, students, parents, and the general public. Write so that someone unfamiliar with your department can understand what you are communicating. Avoid acronyms without explanation and assume a general reading level.

Plain Language

Use plain, direct language. Short sentences are better than long ones. Common words are better than technical jargon. If a sentence requires a second reading to understand, rewrite it.

Structured and Scannable

Most web visitors do not read - they scan. Organize your content so that key information stands out. Use headings, short paragraphs, and bullet points to make scanning easy.

Descriptive Links

Every link should describe where it goes. "Click here" and "read more" do not tell visitors or screen readers anything useful. Instead, use the name of the page or document you are linking to.

Wrong: Click here to download the form.
Right: Download the 2025-2026 Field Trip Request Form.

Professional Formatting

Avoid using ALL CAPS for emphasis - it reads as shouting and reduces readability. Use bold sparingly to highlight genuinely important information. Do not use colored text or multiple font sizes to style content - use the preset heading styles instead.

Page 3 - Proofreading and Approval

Proofreading and Approval

Before any content is published to your department site, it should go through a review process. Publishing unreviewed content increases the risk of errors reaching a district-wide audience.

Basic Proofreading Steps

  • Read the content out loud - this surfaces awkward phrasing and missing words that silent reading misses
  • Check all links - verify that every link opens the correct page or document
  • Verify dates, names, and program titles - these change frequently and are a common source of errors
  • Confirm that any referenced forms or documents are the current versions
  • Check for typos and grammar errors using your browser's spell check or a writing tool

Content Approval

Significant content changes - especially changes to policies, program requirements, contact information, or forms - should be reviewed and approved by the appropriate person in your department before publishing. Consult your department's review process for guidance on what requires sign-off.

A second set of eyes on important content before publishing is one of the most effective ways to prevent errors. Build this into your workflow, even for minor updates.

After Publishing

After publishing a page, view it in the browser to confirm it looks correct. Check formatting, link behavior, and that images are displaying properly before closing the editing session.

Page 4 - Common Content Issues

Common Content Issues

The following content issues are most frequently identified during BCPS department site audits. Each one has a direct impact on the usefulness and credibility of your site.

"Coming Soon" Pages

Pages that say "Coming Soon" or "Content Being Updated" and remain that way for extended periods create a poor visitor experience and suggest the site is not being actively maintained. Either publish the content or remove the page until it is ready.

Broken Links

Broken links are one of the most common and most damaging issues on any website. They indicate that the site is not being maintained and prevent visitors from accessing content they need. Test all links when publishing and during regular audits.

Placeholder Text

Generic placeholder text that was never replaced is occasionally found on department pages during audits. Always verify that new pages do not contain template language that was not updated before publishing.

Excessive PDFs

Using PDFs for all content is a common pattern that creates accessibility problems. PDFs are not always screen-reader compatible, they cannot be resized like web text, and they require a PDF viewer to open. Whenever possible, publish content directly on the page instead of as a PDF.

Duplicate Content

Publishing the same information in multiple places creates maintenance problems. When the information changes, every copy must be updated. Consolidate duplicate content and link to one authoritative source.

ALL CAPS Overuse

Using ALL CAPS for anything other than acronyms reduces readability and comes across as aggressive tone. Use bold or heading styles for emphasis instead.

Page 5 - Content Quality Checklist

Content Quality Checklist

Run through this checklist for any page you are creating or updating. If any item cannot be checked, the page needs attention.

  • All information on this page is accurate and aligned with current district policy
  • All dates, names, and program titles are current
  • All links work and point to the correct destination
  • All links use descriptive text (no "click here" or "read more")
  • No placeholder text remains from a template or previous version
  • No "Coming Soon" sections are left unpublished for extended periods
  • Content is written in plain language appropriate for multiple audiences
  • ALL CAPS is not used for emphasis
  • Bold and italics are used sparingly and purposefully
  • Content that exists elsewhere on the site is linked to rather than duplicated
  • PDFs linked from this page are current and accessible

Page 1 - Why Navigation Matters

Why Navigation Matters

Navigation is the system that helps visitors move through your department site. When it works well, visitors find what they need without frustration. When it does not, even the best content becomes inaccessible.

Poor navigation is one of the top reasons visitors leave a website. It signals that the site is disorganized, and it actively prevents people from completing the tasks they came to do.

What Good Navigation Achieves

  • Visitors can find key content within one or two clicks from the homepage
  • The site structure is predictable - visitors know where to look for different types of content
  • Labels accurately describe the pages they lead to
  • No pages are hidden or inaccessible

Your Navigation Responsibility

As a department WCM, you control the navigation structure of your site. This includes the main menu, dropdown items, and any internal links between pages. Keeping this structure logical and current is part of your ongoing maintenance role.

A visitor should never have to use the browser's back button to find their way around your site. If they do, your navigation needs attention.

Page 2 - Navigation Standards

Navigation Standards

The following standards apply to the navigation structure of all BCPS department sites.

Limit Main Menu Items

The main menu should contain only the most important top-level categories for your site. Most department sites should have between four and seven main menu items. Too many items in the main menu overwhelms visitors and dilutes the importance of each category.

Clear Page Hierarchy

Your site should have a clear hierarchy: a homepage at the top, main sections below, and sub-pages below those. Avoid creating a flat structure where every page sits at the same level with no logical grouping.

Minimize Click Depth

Key content should be reachable within one to two clicks from the homepage. If a visitor has to click more than three times to find important information, that information is too deep in the structure. Consider promoting it or restructuring the navigation.

Descriptive Labels

Navigation labels should describe the content on the destination page. Avoid vague labels like "Resources," "Information," or "More." Use specific labels like "Forms and Documents," "Program Requirements," or "Staff Contacts."

Effective Internal Linking

Beyond the main navigation, use internal links within page content to connect related information. A visitor reading about a program should be able to click directly to the enrollment form, registration deadline, or staff contact without returning to the main menu.

Page 3 - Dropdown Structure

Dropdown Structure

Dropdown menus allow you to group related pages under a main navigation item without cluttering the top-level menu. When used well, they make your site easier to navigate. When overused or poorly organized, they create confusion.

When to Use Dropdowns

Use a dropdown when a main menu item has multiple sub-pages that logically belong together. The sub-pages should all be closely related to the parent item - if they are not, reconsider whether they belong in that group.

Dropdown Limits

Avoid creating dropdowns with more than eight items. Long dropdown menus are difficult to scan and can be hard to interact with on mobile devices. If you have more than eight items in a dropdown, consider whether some should be reorganized, consolidated, or removed.

Nesting

Multi-level dropdowns (dropdowns inside dropdowns) are generally not recommended for department sites. They add complexity without a proportional benefit and perform poorly on mobile screens. Keep your navigation to two levels: main menu items and one level of sub-pages.

Avoid: Creating a dropdown item that leads to a page with no content, or a dropdown where most items link to the same page. Each navigation item should serve a distinct purpose.

Page 4 - Navigation Responsibilities and Audit Prep

Navigation Responsibilities and Audit Preparation

Navigation issues are among the most commonly flagged items during BCPS site audits. Proactive navigation maintenance prevents these issues from accumulating.

Regular Navigation Review

Review your site navigation at least once per semester. Ask:

  • Do all navigation labels still accurately describe their destination pages?
  • Are there pages in the navigation that are outdated, empty, or no longer relevant?
  • Are there important pages on the site that are not reachable from the navigation?
  • Have any programs, services, or departments been renamed that should be reflected in the labels?

Eliminating Duplicate Pages

Over time, sites can accumulate duplicate pages - two or more pages with the same or very similar content. These create confusion in navigation and create a maintenance burden. Identify duplicates and consolidate them into a single authoritative page.

Navigation Checklist

  • Main menu has between four and seven items
  • All navigation labels are descriptive and specific
  • Key content is reachable within two clicks from the homepage
  • No orphaned pages exist (pages not reachable from navigation)
  • No duplicate pages exist
  • Dropdown menus have no more than eight items
  • Navigation reflects current department programs and services

Page 1 - Why Post Boards Matter

Why Post Boards Matter

Post boards are the primary tool for publishing timely news, announcements, and updates on your department site. When used well, they keep your audience informed and demonstrate that your department is actively communicating. When neglected, they make your site feel stale and untrustworthy.

What Post Boards Communicate

An active, well-maintained post board signals that:

  • Your department is engaged and communicating regularly
  • The information on your site is current and being actively managed
  • Visitors can return to your site and expect to find relevant updates

Your Role

As a department WCM, you are responsible for publishing posts in a timely manner, ensuring posts meet content and accessibility standards, organizing posts so they are easy to find, and removing or archiving posts that are no longer relevant.

A post board with the most recent entry dated six months ago suggests the site is not being maintained. Even one or two relevant posts per month is better than silence.

Page 2 - Publishing Standards

Post Publishing Standards

All posts published to your department site must meet the following standards before going live.

Timeliness

Publish posts close to when the event or information becomes relevant. Announcements about upcoming deadlines should be posted with enough lead time for your audience to act. Posts about events that have already passed should be removed or archived promptly.

Clear and Consistent Titles

Every post must have a clear, descriptive title. The title should tell the reader exactly what the post is about. Avoid vague titles like "Important Update" or "New Information." Use specific titles like "2025-2026 Professional Development Calendar Now Available" or "Extended Deadline: Summer Program Applications."

Consistent Formatting

All posts should follow the same formatting pattern: a clear title, an introductory sentence or paragraph summarizing the post, and then supporting detail. Do not mix formatting styles between posts on the same board.

Clear Calls to Action

If a post requires the reader to do something - register, download a form, attend an event - include a clear call to action with a direct link. Do not assume visitors will know what to do with the information.

Audit flag: Posts without clear calls to action or posts with expired links are frequently flagged during content audits. Review all posts regularly for both issues.

Page 3 - Images, Accessibility, and Categorization

Images, Accessibility, and Post Categorization

Using Images in Posts

Images can make posts more visually engaging and help them stand out on a busy board. When using images in posts:

  • Use images that are directly relevant to the post content
  • Use high-quality images that are appropriately sized for web display
  • Always add descriptive alt text to every image (covered in Module 12)
  • Do not use images as the only way to communicate important information - the post text must stand alone

Accessibility in Posts

Post content must meet the same accessibility standards as all other site content. This includes descriptive link text, accessible headings, alt text on images, and no content conveyed through color alone.

Post Categorization and Organization

Categorizing posts makes them easier to find and helps visitors filter for what they need. Assign each post to the most relevant category available on your board. Avoid creating too many categories - a post board with 15 categories is harder to use than one with five well-defined ones.

Review your categories at least once per year to confirm they are still relevant and that no category is empty or has only one post.

Avoid posting duplicate information. If an announcement is already covered in a resource element or on a page, link to that content rather than duplicating it in a post.

Page 1 - What Are Resource Elements?

What Are Resource Elements?

Resource elements are a specific type of content element in Finalsite Composer designed to display links, documents, and files in a structured, visually consistent format. They help visitors quickly identify and access the resources they need.

You have likely seen resource elements on other department pages - they typically appear as a list of linked items with icons indicating the file type (PDF, Word document, link, etc.).

Common Uses for Resource Elements

  • Forms and applications available for download
  • Policy documents and administrative guidelines
  • Program handbooks and reference materials
  • External links to partner organizations or required resources
  • Staff directories with linked contact information

Resource Elements vs. Inline Links

Resource elements are best for pages where visitors expect to find a collection of downloadable materials or links. For individual links within body text, inline hyperlinks are more appropriate. Do not use resource elements as a substitute for all links on a page - use the format that best matches the content and visitor expectation.

Page 2 - Link and Document Standards

Link and Document Standards

Every resource element entry - whether a link or a document - must meet the following standards.

Descriptive Labels

Every link or document in a resource element must have a label that clearly describes what it is. The label should tell visitors what they will get when they click, including the document type and version where relevant.

Wrong: Click here
Wrong: Document 1
Right: 2025-2026 Teacher Evaluation Handbook (PDF)

Logical Organization

Resources within an element should be grouped and ordered logically. Consider alphabetical order for long lists, or topical grouping when resources fall into distinct categories. Add section headers within a resource element when needed to help visitors scan.

Keeping Resources Current

Outdated documents left in resource elements are one of the most common and most harmful content issues on department sites. Visitors may download and use old versions of forms, policies, or guidelines without realizing they are out of date.

  • Review all resource elements at least twice per year
  • Replace outdated documents immediately when new versions are published
  • Remove links to external sites that no longer exist

Avoiding Redundancy

Do not list the same document or link in multiple places on your site unless there is a clear reason for a visitor to find it in both locations. Redundancy creates maintenance problems and can lead to situations where only one copy gets updated.

Page 3 - PDF Management and Accessibility

PDF Management and Accessibility

PDFs are the most common file type linked from BCPS department resource elements. They also represent one of the most significant accessibility risks if not managed carefully.

Accessible PDFs

To be considered accessible, a PDF must meet these minimum requirements:

  • The document is a text-based PDF (not a scanned image of a document)
  • Headings are tagged in the document so screen readers can navigate them
  • Images within the PDF have alt text
  • Reading order is logical when accessed by a screen reader
  • The document language is set correctly in the file properties

If you are unsure whether a PDF is accessible, do not publish it until you have verified. Contact the Office of Communications for guidance on PDF accessibility review.

Managing Document Versions

When you update a document, upload the new version to Finalsite and replace the existing link rather than adding a new one. This ensures visitors always access the current version and prevents old versions from remaining accessible on the site.

When to Use a Page Instead of a PDF

Consider publishing content directly on a web page instead of as a PDF when the information changes frequently, when it contains important links, or when it is content visitors need to read on a screen rather than print.

The Assignment in Module 9 walks you through the process of reviewing a PDF for accessibility. That skill applies directly to every PDF you publish through resource elements.

Page 1 - Why Style Consistency Matters

Why Style Consistency Matters

Visual style is the layer of your site that visitors notice first, often before they read a single word of content. A consistent, professional visual style communicates credibility and signals that your department takes its web presence seriously.

Inconsistent style - mixed font sizes, random use of color, visual elements that do not match - makes a site look unmanaged even when the underlying content is accurate and current.

Brand Consistency

Your department site is part of the BCPS web presence. The style choices you make either reinforce or undermine the district brand. BCPS has an established color palette, typography system, and visual identity that all department sites must follow. These are built into the Finalsite theme - your job is to work within them, not around them.

Your Role in Style Stewardship

Style stewardship means actively maintaining the visual consistency of your site over time. Every time content is added or updated, there is an opportunity for style drift. As a department WCM, you are the first line of defense against that drift.

When in doubt about a style choice, use what is already present on the page. Matching the existing style is almost always the right call.

Page 2 - Font, Text, and Color Standards

Font, Text, and Color Standards

The following standards apply to all text and color choices on your department site.

Font Standards

  • Use only the fonts provided in the Finalsite style system. Do not paste text from Word documents without clearing formatting first, as this can import fonts that are not part of the BCPS design system.
  • Use a single body font size for paragraph text. Do not manually resize body text to create visual hierarchy - use heading styles instead.
  • Avoid using more than two or three type styles on a single page.

Text Formatting

  • Use bold for genuinely important terms or phrases - sparingly, not for decoration
  • Use italics for titles, technical terms, or light emphasis - not for extended passages of text
  • Do not underline text unless it is a hyperlink - underlining implies a link to most visitors
  • Do not use ALL CAPS for anything except acronyms

Color Standards

  • Use only the BCPS approved color palette available in Finalsite
  • Do not use color as the only way to convey information - this fails ADA accessibility standards
  • Ensure sufficient contrast between text color and background color (4.5:1 minimum ratio for standard text)
  • Do not use bright or off-brand colors to draw attention to content - use structural emphasis instead
Page 3 - Strategic Use of Enhancements

Strategic Use of Enhancements

Finalsite Composer provides a variety of visual enhancement elements - feature boxes, callout blocks, icon lists, tabbed content, accordions, and more. These tools are powerful when used with purpose and distracting when overused.

The Rule of Purpose

Before adding any enhancement to a page, ask: does this help the visitor access or understand the content better? If the honest answer is no, do not add it. Enhancements should serve the visitor, not demonstrate that the WCM has explored the available tools.

When Enhancements Work Well

  • Callout boxes: For important notices, tips, or warnings that need to stand apart from body text
  • Accordions: For FAQ sections or content where most visitors only need one or two answers
  • Tabbed content: For separating content by audience type (e.g., students, parents, staff)
  • Icon lists: For visual emphasis on short lists of key points

When Enhancements Hurt

  • Using multiple different enhancement types on the same page creates visual noise
  • Using enhancements for content that would be clearer as simple paragraphs or bullet points
  • Stacking enhancement blocks with no plain content between them

A page with well-placed enhancements looks intentional and professional. A page where every content block is a different visual treatment looks cluttered. Less is more.

Page 4 - Style Checklist

Style Checklist

Use this checklist when creating or reviewing any page. Style issues that pass the WCM review stage are more likely to be flagged during an audit.

  • Only BCPS-approved fonts are used (no imported fonts from Word)
  • Heading styles are used for visual hierarchy, not manual font sizing
  • Bold is used sparingly and for genuinely important terms only
  • No ALL CAPS text except for acronyms
  • No underlined text except for hyperlinks
  • Only BCPS brand colors are used
  • Text contrast meets accessibility requirements
  • Enhancements are used purposefully - each one serves a clear function
  • No more than two or three enhancement types are used on a single page
  • The visual style of this page matches other pages on the site
  • Buttons are styled consistently across the page and the site

Page 1 - Introduction to ADA and Web Accessibility

Introduction to ADA and Web Accessibility

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requires that BCPS websites be accessible to all users, including those who rely on assistive technology such as screen readers, keyboard navigation, and voice control software.

Web accessibility is not optional. For a large district like BCPS, federal accessibility requirements under Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act and WCAG 2.1 AA guidelines apply to all public-facing web content. The federal compliance deadline for large school districts was April 24, 2026. All department websites must meet these standards now.

What Web Accessibility Means in Practice

  • All images have descriptive alt text
  • All links use descriptive text (not "click here")
  • All videos have captions
  • Color is not the only way information is conveyed
  • Pages can be navigated using a keyboard alone
  • All documents (PDFs, Word files) are tagged for accessibility
  • Text has sufficient contrast against its background

Department sites serve a broader audience than school sites, including community members with disabilities who rely on your content for district services and information. ADA compliance is not just a legal requirement - it is a service standard.

Page 2 - What Makes a Page Accessible?

What Makes a Page Accessible?

Accessibility is built into every content decision you make - from the words you choose for link text to the alt text you write for images. The following areas are where most accessibility issues occur on BCPS department sites.

Alternative Text for Images

Every image on your site must have alt text that describes what the image shows. Alt text is read aloud by screen readers for visitors who cannot see the image. If an image is purely decorative, mark it as decorative in Finalsite so screen readers skip it.

Heading Structure

Screen reader users often navigate pages by jumping between headings. This means your heading structure must be logical and complete. Do not skip heading levels. Do not use headings just to make text larger - use them to organize the page.

Link Text

Screen readers can generate a list of all links on a page. If all your links say "click here," that list is useless. Write link text that describes the destination even when read out of context.

Tables

Use tables only for data, not for page layout. Data tables must include a header row and be structured so that the relationship between data and headers is clear. Do not use tables to position text or images on a page.

Color and Contrast

Never use color alone to communicate information. Provide a text label, pattern, or icon alongside any color-coded content. Maintain a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for standard body text and 3:1 for large text.

Page 4 - Resources vs. Galleries

Resources vs. Galleries

Two of the most commonly confused features in Finalsite are Resources and Galleries. Both involve storing and displaying files, but they serve very different purposes.

Resources

Resources are files you upload to Finalsite - PDFs, Word documents, spreadsheets, and other downloadable files. When you link to a downloadable file from a resource element or a text link on your page, you are linking to a Resource stored in Finalsite.

Galleries

Galleries are collections of images displayed visually on a page - typically as a slideshow, grid, or carousel. Galleries are used for photos of events, staff, or department programs where you want visitors to see multiple images together.

  • Use Galleries when you want to display a collection of images for visitors to browse
  • Do not use a Gallery element to host downloadable files, and do not store display images in the Resources folder

Page 4 - Setting Up Layout Pages

  2. Open or create the folder where the file should be stored
  3. Click "Upload" and select the file from your computer
  4. Rename the file if needed - use a descriptive, lowercase name with hyphens instead of spaces (e.g., 2025-2026-field-trip-request.pdf)
  5. Click Save

Linking to an Uploaded File

To link to an uploaded file from a Content Element, highlight the link text in the rich text editor, click the Link button in the toolbar, and select your file from the Resources library.

Replacing a File When a New Version Is Available

When a new version of a document is available, upload the new version to Finalsite and replace the existing file rather than uploading a new copy with a different name. This ensures that all existing links to the file automatically point to the new version.

File naming matters. Use descriptive names that include the school year or version number (e.g., 2025-2026-employee-handbook.pdf). This makes it easy to identify which version is current in the Resources library.

Page 6 - Tips for Content Editing

Tips for Content Editing

These practical tips will help you work more efficiently and avoid common mistakes when editing content in Finalsite Composer.

Clear Formatting When Pasting from Word or Google Docs

When pasting text from Microsoft Word, Google Docs, or another program, always use the "Paste as Plain Text" option in Finalsite's rich text editor (usually Ctrl+Shift+V or a toolbar button). Pasting directly with formatting can import incorrect fonts, colors, and spacing that conflict with your site's style.

Use Undo Generously

If you make a change that does not look right, use Ctrl+Z to undo before saving. Once you save, you cannot undo changes through the keyboard shortcut - you would need to manually revert the content.

Save Before Previewing

Always save your changes before using the Preview function. The preview shows the saved version of the page, not unsaved edits.

Do Not Leave Pages in Draft for Extended Periods

Draft pages that sit unpublished for weeks or months become outdated before they are ever seen. If a page is not ready to publish, note why in a comment or team communication and set a target publish date.

Check Your Work on Multiple Browsers

After publishing a page, view it in both Chrome and Edge (or Safari on Mac) to confirm it displays consistently. Most visitors use Chrome, but your audience may use any browser.

Page 7 - Paragraph Styles

Paragraph Styles

Finalsite's rich text editor includes a Paragraph Styles dropdown that applies preset formatting combinations to selected text. Using paragraph styles - instead of manually adjusting font size, weight, and color - ensures your content follows the BCPS design system consistently.

Available Styles

  • Heading 2: Major section headings on a page
  • Heading 3: Sub-section headings within a major section
  • Heading 4: Minor sub-headings when additional levels are needed
  • Body / Paragraph: Standard body text - the default style for most content
  • Callout or Intro Text: A larger body style for introductory paragraphs (where available in your template)

When Not to Use Manual Formatting

Do not manually increase font size to simulate a heading. Do not use the font color tool to style text. These approaches create inconsistency and may break accessibility standards. Always use the Paragraph Styles dropdown to apply formatting.

If the style you need is not available in the dropdown, contact the Office of Communications. Do not work around a missing style by applying manual formatting.

Page 8 - Images and Tables

Images and Tables

Adding Images to a Content Element

  1. Place your cursor where you want the image to appear in the Content Element
  2. Click the Image button in the rich text editor toolbar
  3. Select an image from the Finalsite media library, or upload a new one
  4. Add descriptive alt text in the Alt Text field - this is required for every image
  5. Set the image size and alignment appropriate for your layout
  6. Click Insert

Image Best Practices

  • Use images that are relevant to the content - not decorative stock photos that add no information
  • Optimize images for web before uploading (maximum 200KB for most content images)
  • Do not stretch or distort images from their original proportions
  • Use .jpg for photographs and .png for graphics with transparent backgrounds

Using Tables

Tables must be used only for data - never for page layout. A data table presents information with a clear relationship between rows and columns. Examples: a schedule showing days and times, a comparison of program requirements, a fee schedule.

Every data table must have a header row. Mark the header row as a header in the table settings so that screen readers correctly associate data cells with their column or row headers.

Do not use tables to control the visual position of text or images on a page. This is a layout-through-table approach that was common in early web design but creates serious accessibility and mobile display problems. Use Layout Pages or proper content elements instead.

Page 1 - Adding Alt Text

Adding Alt Text to Images

Alt text (alternative text) is a written description of an image that is read aloud by screen readers for users who cannot see the image. It is also displayed when an image fails to load. Every image on your department site must have appropriate alt text - no exceptions.

How to Write Good Alt Text

  • Describe the image as you would to someone who cannot see it
  • Be specific but concise - one or two sentences is usually sufficient
  • Include information that is relevant to the context of the page
  • Do not start with "Image of..." or "Photo of..." - screen readers already announce that it is an image
  • If the image contains text, include that text in the alt text

Decorative Images

If an image is purely decorative and adds no information to the page - a divider line, a generic background image - mark it as decorative in Finalsite's image settings. Screen readers will then skip it, which is the correct behavior for a purely decorative element.

Examples

Weak: "Students in a classroom"
Better: "Department of Academic Programs staff presenting at the 2025 Summer Professional Development Conference"

Alt text that is genuinely descriptive and contextually relevant is one of the highest-impact accessibility improvements you can make to your department site. Do not write "image" or leave the field blank.

Page 2 - Link Language

Link Language

The text used for hyperlinks - called anchor text or link language - is one of the most important accessibility elements on a web page. Screen reader users can navigate by jumping from link to link, which means each link must make sense even when read out of context.

Rules for Link Text

  • Never use "click here," "read more," "learn more," or "here" as link text
  • Never use a bare URL as link text (e.g., www.browardschools.com/forms)
  • Use the name of the destination page or document
  • For documents, include the file type in parentheses: "2025-2026 Field Trip Request Form (PDF)"
  • For external links, consider noting that the link opens an external site

Links That Open in a New Tab

Links that open in a new browser tab or window should be clearly indicated so users know what to expect. In the link settings, if you select "Open in new tab," add "(opens in new tab)" or a similar note to the link text or a screen-reader-accessible label.

Testing Your Links

After publishing a page, click every link to verify it opens the correct destination. A working link on a draft page can break after publishing if the linked document has been moved or the linked page URL has changed.
